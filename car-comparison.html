About this model

This tool compares the total net cost of ownership for a used RAV4 (gas) vs. a used Model Y (EV).

I currently own the RAV4 (2013), and the earliest/cheapest model Y is 2020/2021.

Up-front costs include taxes and registration, estimated at 10% the purchase price of the vehicle.

Depreciation uses an exponential declining-balance model: the car loses a fixed percentage of its current value each year (not straight line depreciation). This means depreciation costs are front-loaded and slow down over time.

Cost of capital (Invest the difference) assumes you take the difference in the purchase price of the two vehicles, and invest the difference if purchasing the cheaper option. This creates a fair apples-to-apples comparison by estimating the cost of capital (or, in a positive sense, the return on investment of the capital difference)

Maintenance costs are typically lower for EVs (no oil changes, less frequent brake replacement due to regenerative braking, etc), and modeled here as a cost per mile driven, using estimated values from an internet search (10c/mi ICE, 6c/mi EV)

Fuel obviously the biggest differentiator between gas and electric. EV charging must be sub-categorized into supercharging (away from home, most expensive), home charging (base utility rate), and home solar generation (nearly free, see below). Select the percentage of charging done at home (grid and home solar), with the remainder covered by supercharging.

Home solar adds nuance: under Net Energy Metering 2.0 (NEM), surplus energy exported to the grid over a 12-month cycle is compensated via Net Surplus Compensation (NSC). The surplus is credited and valued at wholesale energy prices, approx 2.8c/kWh. This is much lower than the retial paid to purchase energy from the utility, meaning that any net annual surplus solar energy production is essentially free EV charging (it has next to no residual value if un-used).
